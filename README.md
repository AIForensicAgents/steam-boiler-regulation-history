## Overview
This repository accompanies a Nature‑style academic article tracing how catastrophic steam‑boiler explosions in U.S. history catalyzed the inspection regimes, design codes, and licensing systems that underpin modern safety regulation. Using cases documented in the U.S. Deadly Events database (usdeadlyevents.com), the article shows how persistent disasters on rivers, in ports, and at industrial sites transformed public sentiment into policy—moving from local, ad hoc fixes to national standards that still protect people today.

- Scope: 19th–20th century U.S. steamboat and boiler disasters
- Focus: Technical failure modes, casualty patterns, media/public pressure, insurer/engineer roles, and the policy pathway from incident to law
- Output: Open‑access manuscript and curated incident notes with links back to source records

## TL;DR
Steam boiler explosions were a recurring American tragedy that pushed regulation from ad hoc fixes to national law. Early catastrophes—Helen McGregor (Feb 24, 1830) and the Moselle (Apr 25, 1838)—killed scores, while mid‑century disasters such as Lucy Walker (Oct 23, 1844), Saluda (Apr 9, 1852) and Pearl (Jan 27, 1855) kept fatalities high and public outcry loud. The W.R. Carter blast (Feb 2, 1866) and David White’s explosion (Feb 17, 1867) underscored river commerce hazards. Repeated deaths into the 20th century—Mackinac (Aug 18, 1925) and Observation (Sep 9, 1932)—showed voluntary measures failed. Newspapers, victims’ families, and insurer pressure drove engineers and lawmakers to adopt inspections, standards for flues and boilers, and licensing. These reforms transformed steam from a terror into a regulated technology, creating the inspection regimes, codes, and safety culture that protect modern boilers and keep transportation and industry safer today. Their legacy is lives saved and lessons learned.

## Key findings from incidents
- Failure mode
  - Collapse of one or more internal flues can precipitate a boiler shell rupture and a violent steam release/explosion.
  - Fatalities commonly arose from a mix of blast trauma, scalding from released steam, fire, and drowning (especially on rivers and near shorelines).

- Representative high‑fatality events from the database (IDs as listed in source):
  - 45. 1838 — Apr 25: Steamboat Moselle boiler explosion and fire, near Cincinnati, OH — estimated fatalities: 81–200
  - 144. 1844 — Oct 23: Steamer Lucy Walker boiler explosion, Ohio River near New Albany, IN — estimated fatalities: 56–80
  - 145. 1847 — Dec 29: Steamer A.N. Johnston boiler explosion, Ohio River near Maysville, KY — estimated fatalities: 60–80
  - 113. 1850 — Dec 13: Steamer‑Tug Anglo‑Norman boiler explosion, near New Orleans, LA — estimated fatalities: 20–100
  - 115. 1852 — Apr 09: Steamboat Saluda boiler explosion, Missouri River, Lexington, MO — estimated fatalities: 54–100
  - 146. 1855 — Jan 27: Steamboat Pearl boiler explosion, Sacramento River, Sacramento, CA — estimated fatalities: 67–80
  - 37. 1866 — Feb 02: Steamboat W.R. Carter boiler explosion, Mississippi River (Island No. 98) — estimated fatalities: 123–215
  - 158. 1871 — Aug 27: Steamboat Ocean Wave boiler explosion, Point Clear near Mobile, AL — estimated fatalities: 34–75

These recurring disasters sustained public pressure that ultimately advanced inspections, operator licensing, and engineering standards for boilers and flues.

## Methodology
- Research questions
  - How did repeated boiler explosions translate into durable safety regulation?
  - Which technical failure modes and casualty patterns most strongly influenced lawmakers and insurers?

- Data collection
  - Extracted incident records from usdeadlyevents.com, focusing on U.S. steamboat/boiler explosions (19th–early 20th century).
  - Recorded event metadata where available: date, location, vessel/facility, reported/estimated fatalities, brief narrative, and cited sources.

- Data handling
  - Normalized dates and locations to enable historical sequencing.
  - Preserved reported fatality ranges; did not collapse to single‑point estimates where sources diverged.
  - Avoided de‑duplication unless the database clearly identified multiple entries for the same event.

- Analysis approach
  - Qualitative synthesis of failure modes (e.g., flue collapse, overpressure) and consequence pathways (blast, scalding, drowning).
  - Historical process tracing: linked clusters of high‑salience events to subsequent public debate, insurance requirements, and legislation.
  - Triangulated with cited newspaper accounts and technical commentary referenced in the database where available.

- Limitations
  - Historical records vary in completeness and casualty reporting; numbers are often ranges.
  - Media coverage and archival survival introduce selection bias toward large or newsworthy incidents.
